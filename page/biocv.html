Bio

Michelle Chen is a visual artist working in Sculpture and Illustration to question the Chinese-American narrative and the human-nature relationship. She is graduating from UC Berkeley with a degree in Art Practice. Her further interests include examining ornamental in regards to utilitarianism, aesthetics and mundanity, and digitalized nature.

Artist Statement

The popular mind defines "Chinese" with hate, anger, and mistrust. Amidst the resurgence of “Asian-as-diseased” stereotypes and the continually deterioration of China-US relations, it is hard to heal this divide. Else, we fetishize "Asian" as an Orientalist aesthetic—opulent, ornamental and portable—reducing the human experience to a mere decoration. However, to be human is to be multi-dimensional: there is no “one” Asian as there is no “one” human. As the world grows smaller, all the closer we are to each other and it becomes all the more apparent that we need to recognize and dismantle these reductive standards. In modernity, we are all humans.

Furthermore, nature is always framed as a "human discovery" when--in reality--there is nothing to be discovered--only categorized and put into a place. As seductive it is to be the dominating power in nature, we have destroyed the very place we live. Climate change, ocean acidification, consumerism, and more, all self-inflicted by-products of the human-nature imperialistic relationship. To become sustainable is to redefine this hierarchy into a form of coexistence and equality.

Proposed projects include: a kinetic crane sculpture (taking the symbol of longevity made out of discarded waste, pitting aesthetic against consumerism), digital ecosystem installations (real-time human disturbance in a "play" environment) and modernized landscape prints (the idealized world overlaid with modern obstructions).
