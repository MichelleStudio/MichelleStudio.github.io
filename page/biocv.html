Bio

Michelle Chen is a visual artist working in Sculpture and Illustration to question engrained traditions and how–if at all–they are still applicable to modern life. She is graduating from UC Berkeley with a degree in Art Practice. Her further interests include the role of humanity in relation to nature, functionality in the presence of aesthetics, and the function of waste generated from a consumer economy.

Artist Statement

My work focuses on traditional Asian motifs, questioning their place in the context of the modern world. Symbols show what a society has historically valued and honored, distilling complex concepts into easily propagatable forms. They express what is supposed to be desirable and noteworthy, but are fundamentally flawed by their reliance on retrospective reasoning. There is no room to consider the prospective future. In this way, cultural motifs can only champion its past. Modern Chinese society still heavily relies on these symbols in everyday life–for better or for worse. Should we stand by these interpretations, or have they gained new meanings in the modern era? It is within this gray area that my work explores. I often work with experimental media arts, blending together technical elements–electronics, code, physics, etc….– with more traditional art forms such as illustration and photography. At the moment, I have been working on a kinetic crane sculpture, questioning the symbol of longevity in the consumer society we find ourselves in today.
